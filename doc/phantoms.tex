\section{simple shapes}
Four simple shapes were employed, based on standard measures on Euclidean space:
\begin{description}
\item[scalar] $f \left( \mathbf{x} \right) = 1$
\item[diamond] $f \left( \mathbf{x} \right) = \begin{cases} 1, & \left\| \mathbf{x} \right\|_1 = \left| x_1 \right| + \left| x_2 \right| + \ldots + \left| x_n \right| \le 1 \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$
\item[sphere] $f \left( \mathbf{x} \right) = \begin{cases} 1, & \left\| \mathbf{x} \right\|_2 = \left( x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \ldots + x_n^2 \right)^\frac{1}{2} \le 1 \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$
\item[cube] $f \left( \mathbf{x} \right) = \begin{cases} 1, & \left\| \mathbf{x} \right\|_\infty = \max \left\{ x_1 , x_2, \ldots , x_n \right\} \le 1 \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$
\end{description}

\section{bell}
A broad, smooth bell-shaped object was also employed. This function peaks at the origin, and tapers to zero at the farthest corner from the origin. If $\mathbf{c}$ is any corner of the grid, then we describe the intensity by the function $\cos^2 \left( \frac{\pi}{2} \frac{\left\| \mathbf{x} \right\|}{\left\| \mathbf{c} \right\|}\right)$.
